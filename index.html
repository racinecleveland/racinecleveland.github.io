Howdy!

I'm Racine Cleveland, a planetary science post-doctoral research fellow at Boise State University under the advisement of Dr. Brian Jackson in the Physics Department. I just recently defended my PhD thesis in Space and Planetary Science at the University of Arkansas titled "Of Dunes and Ice: A Surface–Atmosphere Investigation of Mars CO2 Sublimation Features and Titan Aeolian Fields". My research focused on Mars geomorphology, where I analyzed high-resolution HiRISE imagery to study sublimation-driven surface changes in the planet’s south polar region. I also worked with radar remote sensing, comparing backscatter data from Titan’s hydrocarbon dunes and Earth’s silicate dunes. My current research involves terrestrial and Martian dust devil physics. By combining Earth based field work with planetary in-situ results, we are able to gain cross-planetary results about the same feature. Beyond research, I’m actively involved in science outreach. I am funded by the IDAstro program which is an NSF funded, multi-college outreach program with Bruneau Dunes State Park in south-central Idaho. This program was created with the idea of establishing collaboration between the colleges of southern Idaho and the state park observatory, as well as promoting science education with an emphasis on Astronomy and Astrophysics. (Grant number 2424470) With a background in science and a passion for planetary exploration, I’m always looking for new ways to apply remote sensing and geospatial analysis to better understand our solar system!
